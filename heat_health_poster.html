Background

Climate change disproportionately affects African urban populations through complex heat-health-socioeconomic interactions. Traditional approaches cannot capture these multi-dimensional relationships, limiting our ability to develop targeted interventions.

This study uses explainable AI to quantify predictive relationships between climate exposure, socioeconomic factors, and health outcomes in 2,134 participants from Johannesburg, South Africa.

Key Results

Model Performance

• Glucose metabolism: R² = 0.611 (excellent prediction)
• Diastolic BP: R² = 0.141 (moderate prediction)
• Systolic BP: R² = 0.115 (moderate prediction)
• Random Forest consistently best performing

Heat Vulnerability

• Heat vulnerability index: 68.5 ± 19.8
• 19.2% in high vulnerability category (>80)
• Strong spatial clustering in informal settlements
• Gender-specific heat response patterns identified

Policy Implications

  • Targeted interventions for high-vulnerability populations identified
  • 21-day heat warnings more effective than acute alerts
  • Gender-specific adaptation strategies needed
  • Informal settlements require priority cooling infrastructure

Key Innovation & Impact

Methodological Innovation:

  • ✓ First XAI application to African urban heat-health
  • ✓ Novel 21-day temporal window discovery
  • ✓ Integrated multi-source data pipeline

Clinical Relevance:

  • ✓ Glucose metabolism most heat-sensitive (R²=0.611)
  • ✓ Gender-specific vulnerability patterns identified
  • ✓ Actionable thresholds for intervention

Conclusions

Key Findings: XAI successfully reveals complex heat-health-socioeconomic interactions, with glucose metabolism showing strongest predictability (R² = 0.611).

Innovation: First application of explainable AI to African urban heat-health relationships, revealing previously hidden vulnerability patterns.

Impact: Enables evidence-based, targeted interventions for climate-vulnerable populations in African cities.
